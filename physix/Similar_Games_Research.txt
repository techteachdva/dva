1. GAME OVERVIEW & RESEARCH SCOPE
================================================================================

Games Analyzed:
- Slope (Y8/Addicting Games, endless runner)
- Snow Rider 3D (2020 web endless runner)
- Rolling Sky (Cheetah Games, rhythm-tile runner)
- Rollance (physics-focused mobile ball-roller)

All four games share the core "descend a slope while dodging obstacles"
mechanic with physics-driven momentum. Their design solutions for difficulty,
progression, and obstacle vocabulary provide a blueprint for refining Physix.

2. SLOPE GAME ANALYSIS
================================================================================

Core Mechanics
--------------
- Endless procedural descent. No levels, no finish line.
- Ball accelerates continuously via gravity.
- Controls: Left/Right arrow keys only. No jump. No brake.
- Death is instant on any collision.
- Score = distance traveled.

Difficulty Scaling
------------------
The primary difficulty mechanic is GRADUAL SPEED INCREASE:
- Early phase (0-30 points): Low speed, forgiving turns.
- Mid phase (30-100): Speed requires constant micro-adjustments.
- High phase (100-200): Oversteering is the #1 cause of death.
- Expert (200+): Frame-perfect reactions required.

This creates an organic skill-matching system without manual difficulty
settings. The player is always in a "flow state" where challenge matches
improving reflexes.

Obstacle Vocabulary (Pattern Language)
---------------------------------------
Slope uses a fixed set of recurring obstacle ARRANGEMENTS rather than
fully random chaos:

  Slants        - Angled platforms that launch the ball unpredictably.
                  High-level players avoid center, ride side ramps.
  Speedtunnels  - Ramped tunnels that accelerate the ball further.
  Snakes        - Winding narrow paths requiring smooth arcs.
  Tunnels       - Enclosed passages with ramps.
                  Advanced players use "skips" to drop between sections.
  Treblocks     - Block fields with tiny gaps.
                  Frame-perfect "Treblock Skips" exist for speedrunners.
  Verts         - Vertical barriers that BEGIN MOVING 3 seconds after
                  spawning. High-score runners intentionally crash into
                  idle Verts to execute "Vert Slowdowns" (controlled stop
                  to regain control at extreme speed).
  RNG Blocks    - Randomly generated block fields.
                  CRITICAL DESIGN DETAIL: they NEVER spawn on the extreme
                  right edge, making the right edge the safest high-speed
                  line.

Key Design Insight:
Every obstacle has exploitable properties that reward deep system mastery.
The game is 100% skill-based with no power-ups or luck.

Visual Design
-------------
- Neon high-contrast palette (no visual noise).
- Clean geometry so depth perception and timing are the only skills tested.
- Minimalist aesthetic serves the mechanics.

3. SNOW RIDER 3D ANALYSIS
================================================================================

Core Mechanics
--------------
- Endless procedural sled descent (late 2020 web game).
- Automatic forward momentum (gravity-driven, no acceleration button).
- Controls: Left/Right steering; Up/Space to jump.
- Death = collision OR falling off cliff.
- Score = pure distance.

Difficulty Scaling
------------------
- Dynamic difficulty tied to survival time.
- After ~20-40 seconds, speed crosses a threshold where players must shift
  from coin collection to pure survival.
- High-level play = reading terrain patterns seconds ahead.
- "Wide arc" turns are safer than sharp flicks at high speed.

Obstacle Catalog
----------------
  Trees / Tall Pines    - Clusters that block forward view.
  Rocks / Boulders      - Force tight weaving, create narrow lanes.
  Snowmen               - Tall figures with tiny gaps.
  Rolling Snowballs     - Moving hazards with shifting gaps.
  Wooden Fences         - Long barriers that trick depth perception.
  Roots & Stumps        - Low hazards requiring jumps.
  Gaps & Cliffs         - Sudden drops, sometimes hidden behind trees.
  Cabin Structures      - Large solid objects that break up path width.

Visual Clarity
--------------
- High-contrast winter theme: crisp white snow vs brown/tan obstacles.
- Mountain walls frame the play area.
- Center position is tactically dominant.

Risk/Reward Placement
---------------------
- Gift boxes (currency for sled cosmetics) are deliberately placed in
  dangerous spots: between trees, near cliff edges, after tight passages.
- Forces players to choose between safe passage and cosmetic unlocks.

Sources:
- https://ridersnow3d.com/snow-rider-3d-complete-player-guide/

4. ROLLING SKY ANALYSIS
================================================================================

Core Mechanics
--------------
- Tap-to-steer ball across 5-lane tiled paths.
- Not a pure physics sim, but uses momentum and timing heavily.
- Levels are HAND-CRAFTED (not procedural), set to music.

Difficulty Rating System
-------------------------
6-tier star system:
  1 - Very Easy  (Cloud, Sunny, Massif)
  2 - Easy       (Sky, Laser, Football Field)
  3 - Normal     (Forest, Candy, Kung Fu)
  4 - Hard       (Volcano, City, Tetris)
  5 - Very Hard  (Deep Space, Egypt, Halloween)
  6 - Extremely Hard (Cube, Happy Birthday, Halloween Night)

Community uses DECIMAL ratings (5.8, 6.9) for "perfect way" difficulty
(collecting all diamonds and crowns).

Dual-Layered Difficulty:
- "Easy way" = survive to the end.
- "Perfect way" = collect all collectibles while surviving.
- A 1-star level can have a 2-star+ perfect path.

Obstacle Design
---------------
General Obstacles (reused across levels):
  Riser, Air Crusher, Hammers, Lasers, Wheels, Blocks (1x1 to 1x3),
  Floaters, Slashers, Trees.

Themed Obstacles (unique to level themes):
  Halloween: Tombstone (bumper-type), Pumpkin Laser
  Deep Space: Space Riser, Space Wheel, Space Gate
  Christmas: Gift Riser, Candy Cane Slasher, Bell Archway
  Kung Fu: Panda Fighter, Shinto Gate, Baton Slasher

Key Design Mechanics
--------------------
- Cycling Jumps: Jump pads move across lanes in fixed sequences
  (1->3->5->2->4), requiring rhythmic pattern memorization.
- Hitbox Precision: Obstacles rated by Area x Height (1x1, 1x3, 3x3).
- Speed Variation: Some levels are easier purely due to slower speed.
- Diagonal Movement: Higher difficulties require diagonal tile movements.
- Short but Hard: Some of the shortest levels are the hardest
  (Clown Park, Football Field).

5. PHYSICS-BASED ROLLING GAME BEST PRACTICES
================================================================================

From developer postmortems of Rollance, Crumble, and other titles:

Design Philosophy: Commit to Physics Identity
---------------------------------------------
- Decide early: realistic physics vs arcade physics.
- Realistic physics takes at least 2x longer to tune but creates emergent
  gameplay players cannot find elsewhere.
- Rollance succeeded by focusing on realistic ball physics, attracting a
  loyal, high-retention audience.

Difficulty Progression: Underestimate the Learning Curve
----------------------------------------------------------
- Players do NOT master momentum quickly. Early levels must be VERY forgiving.
- Flatten the early curve: wider platforms, fewer punishment drops,
  more recovery routes.
- Smooth curve with intermittent spikes (like Getting Over It / Jump King).
- Before demanding perfect timing, teach the concept in a forgiving context.

Momentum as Primary Currency
-----------------------------
- Verticality must be built around momentum shaping, not just positioning.
- Sloped terrain and curved walls should redirect rolling momentum into
  upward lift naturally.
- Every significant punishment needs a possible save mechanism.
- Fall-catcher zones (concave slopes, side flippers, bumpers) catch
  failing players.
- "Every save needs to feel like a win."

Checkpoint & Failure Design
---------------------------
- Physics games have high randomness. Optional checkpoints reduce
  frustration for casual players.
- Lock prestige rewards (ranks, S+ medals) when checkpoints are used.
- Preserve hardcore experience while keeping game accessible.
- Core playtesting question: "Did the player feel they failed because of
  the game, or because they weren't good enough yet?"

Content Variety via Physics
---------------------------
- Expand through physics mechanics (materials, surfaces, friction zones)
  rather than scripted events.
- Rollance added ball material switching (wood, metal, paper) and saw
  major metric improvements.
- Avoid visual clutter; cosmetic skins may not resonate if they distract
  from physical feedback.

Playtesting & Metrics
---------------------
- Extensive playtesting is non-negotiable for physics games.
- Observe where players restart, where they audibly react, how long
  they stay in each zone.
- Rollance expanded from 20 to 50+ levels. Result:
  * Day 1 retention: +10 percentage points
  * Day 30 retention: 4.2%
  * Average daily playtime: 15 minutes

Production Best Practices
-------------------------
- Budget for physics complexity (2x production time vs scripted levels).
- Plan iterative tuning of surface friction, ball mass, gravity, collision.
- Adaptive graphics presets maintain smooth framerate (boosts LTV ~20%).
- Granular difficulty settings (time scale, recovery windows) widen audience.

6. KEY TAKEAWAYS & RECOMMENDATIONS FOR PHYSIX
================================================================================

IMMEDIATE IMPROVEMENTS
----------------------
1. SPEED SCALING
   - Current levels have fixed forward speed. Add a small automatic
     acceleration over time (or per section) so later parts of a level
     feel faster than the beginning. This is Slope's #1 difficulty tool.

2. OBSTACLE PATTERN LANGUAGE
   - Instead of placing random bumpers and wind zones, design RECURRING
     ARRANGEMENTS that players can learn to recognize:
     * "Snake" - alternating left/right bumpers in sequence
     * "Tunnel" - wind zones on both sides creating a narrow safe corridor
     * "Slalom" - ice patches between bumpers requiring careful steering
     * "Gauntlet" - rapid-fire bumper sequence with tiny gaps
   - Rolling Sky shows that hand-crafted obstacle sequences feel fairer
     than pure randomness.

3. RISK/REWARD COIN PLACEMENT
   - Place coins in dangerous positions: near bumpers, in narrow passages,
     after speed boosters where momentum makes collection harder.
   - Snow Rider 3D's gift box placement is the model here.

4. THEME-BASED OBSTACLE VARIATIONS
   - Rolling Sky reuses the same HITBOX LOGIC across levels but reskins
     the visuals per world. Physix can do the same:
     * World 1 (Beginner): Simple blocks, gentle curves
     * World 2 (Friction): Ice patches, low-friction surfaces
     * World 3 (Gravity): Gravity zones with visual color coding
     * World 4 (Momentum): Heavy bumpers, moving platforms
     * World 5 (Quantum): All mechanics combined + invisibility/teleport?

DIFFICULTY PROGRESSION
----------------------
5. WIDER EARLY PLATFORMS, NARROW LATE PASSAGES
   - Best practice: start with track width 8-10, gradually narrow to 6
     or even 5 in late-game sections. Combined with curves, this creates
     genuine choke points.

6. OPTIONAL CHECKPOINTS
   - Add visible checkpoint zones that players pass through.
   - If they die, respawn at last checkpoint (lives still consumed).
   - Hardcore players can aim for "No-Checkpoint" medals.

7. DUAL-LAYER DIFFICULTY
   - Easy path = finish the level.
   - Perfect path = collect all coins while finishing.
   - Star rating should depend on coins collected AND time AND deaths.

8. SAVE MECHANICS (Fall-Catchers)
   - In curved sections where players might fly off, add gentle concave
     slopes or side rails that catch the ball and bounce it back toward
     center rather than instant death.
   - "Every significant punishment needs a possible save."

LONG-TERM CONTENT
-----------------
9. MATERIAL SWITCHING (SHOP ITEM)
   - Rollance saw metric improvements with ball materials:
     * Rubber = high friction, low bounce
     * Metal = heavy, maintains momentum
     * Glass = slippery, fragile (one hit = break?)
     * Bouncy ball = high bounce off bumpers
   - This is a natural shop expansion that actually changes physics.

10. PROCEDURAL BONUS LEVELS
    - The current bonus levels are straight lines. Make them procedural:
      generate obstacle sequences from the pattern vocabulary.
    - Slope and Snow Rider both use procedural generation to ensure
      replayability.

11. SPEED-BASED STAR RATINGS
    - Current star formula is mostly time + coins + deaths.
    - Add a "speed maintained" bonus: if the player stays above a
      threshold speed for long sections, bonus points.

VISUAL & AUDIO
--------------
12. HIGH-CONTRAST OBSTACLE COLOR CODING
    - Every obstacle type should have a consistent color:
      * Bumpers = red
      * Wind zones = cyan
      * Ice = light blue
      * Gravity zones = purple
      * Boost pads = green
      * Moving platforms = orange
    - Players should be able to identify hazards instantly at high speed.

13. MUSIC-SYNCED OBSTACLES (Rolling Sky Model)
    - In later worlds, moving platforms and cycling hazards could
      animate to the music beat. This creates a rhythm-game feel and
      makes patterns predictable.
